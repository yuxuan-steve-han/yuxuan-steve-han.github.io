Rats, Spiders, and a Cheese Hat: Post-Mortem of Nibbles and Nightmares

Six of us built a local 2-player asymmetric action game for Jam o' Lantern 2024. One player is a rat trying to steal a cheese hat. The other is a spider trying to stop them. Here is how it went.

The Setup

Jam o' Lantern 2024 was a Halloween-themed game jam, and our team of six decided to go all in on the spirit of it. The team was split across disciplines: four programmers (myself, Angela Rhee, Ryan Choi, and Andrew Zhang), Shiori Thompson on music and sound design, and Aasiya D'Urso and Vicky Xue handling all of the art. Having six people on a jam team is unusual. Most jam teams are two to four. The larger team gave us more bandwidth, but it also made coordination significantly harder.

The concept we landed on was an asymmetric local multiplayer game set in a sewer. Player one controls a rat navigating through the sewer system, collecting items, and trying to reach the legendary Cheese Hat. Player two controls a spider whose job is to stop the rat by any means available: shooting webs to slow the rat down, picking up items, and teleporting the cheese to a new location. The two players share a screen and use different control schemes.

The main menu screen. It reads "Cheese Rat Game" with Play, Option, and Quit buttons. The grey placeholder background is a telling artifact of jam development: all the art time went into gameplay sprites and environments, leaving the menu as functional programmer UI. It gets you into the game. That is all it needed to do.

The rat uses WASD to move, E to pick up items, and F to use them. The spider uses arrow keys to move, left click to shoot webs, right shift to pick up items, right control to use items, and numpad enter to teleport the cheese. If you are already noticing that the spider's control scheme has a lot of keys spread across the keyboard, that is one of the things we will get to in the "what went wrong" section.

What Went Right

1. The asymmetric design created natural tension

The best decision we made was making the two players fundamentally different rather than giving them mirrored abilities. The rat is fast and mobile but fragile. The spider is slower but has powerful disruption tools: web shooting for area denial and the ability to teleport the cheese hat to a completely different part of the map. This created a dynamic where the rat player had to plan routes and react quickly, while the spider player had to predict the rat's path and set traps.

I play Overwatch competitively at the varsity level, and the asymmetric hero design in that game directly influenced how I thought about Nibbles and Nightmares. In Overwatch, the most interesting matchups happen when two characters have completely different toolkits interacting in unexpected ways. A Tracer versus a McCree plays nothing like a Tracer versus a Reinhardt. We wanted that same feeling of fundamentally different approaches colliding. Making one player the pursuer and the other the evader, rather than two characters with the same abilities racing to the same objective, made the game feel like a cat-and-mouse chase rather than a mirror match.

2. Art direction was strong from the start

Having two dedicated artists was a luxury most jam teams do not have, and it showed. Aasiya and Vicky established the visual style early: dark, moody sewer environments with characters that popped against the background. The rat and spider sprites were expressive and distinct even at small resolutions. The cheese hat itself was immediately recognizable as the objective, which meant we did not need extensive UI to communicate the goal to players. You see a glowing cheese hat at the end of a sewer tunnel. You know what to do.

Split-screen gameplay in action. Left panel: the rat with the cheese hat on its back, navigating the sewer. Right panel: the spider closing in. The green sewer environment, grey stone walls, and scattered items (webs, collectibles) are visible. The 00:29 timer at the top adds pressure. Notice how the character sprites read clearly against the environment even in a split-screen view at this scale.

The art also set the tone for the entire game. The character designs were deliberately silly (a rat in a sewer desperate for a hat made of cheese, hunted by a spider), which gave us permission to lean into absurd game mechanics without worrying about tonal consistency. The humor was baked into the visuals, so the gameplay could focus on being fun rather than trying to be funny.

3. Sound design elevated the experience

Shiori handled all music and sound, and the audio work punched well above what you would expect from a jam game. The ambient sewer soundscape gave the environment a sense of place. The musical cues shifted based on proximity to the cheese hat, creating tension without the player consciously noticing why they felt more stressed. Sound effects for web shooting, item pickups, and the cheese teleport were distinct and satisfying. Audio is often an afterthought in jam games, and having a dedicated person on it from day one made a noticeable difference in how polished the game felt.

What Went Wrong

1. The spider's control scheme was too complex

This was our biggest design failure. The rat's controls are clean: WASD, E, F. Three clusters of keys, all on the left side of the keyboard. The spider's controls are spread across the entire keyboard and mouse: arrow keys for movement, left click for web, right shift for item pickup, right control for item use, numpad enter for cheese teleport. During playtesting after the jam, almost every spider player had to look at the controls reference at least three times before they could play comfortably. Several players gave up on playing spider entirely.

The root cause was that we designed the spider's abilities first and then tried to map them to available keys after the rat player had claimed WASD and E/F. We should have designed the control scheme holistically from the start, or reduced the spider's ability count to fit a simpler control layout. Two abilities on the spider instead of three would have allowed a much cleaner mapping.

Lesson

For local multiplayer games sharing a single keyboard (and mouse), design the control schemes for both players simultaneously, not sequentially. The second player's controls are not an afterthought. They are half of the game's user experience.

2. Four programmers without clear ownership boundaries

Four programmers on a jam team sounds like an advantage. In practice, it created coordination overhead that ate into productive development time. With two programmers, you can split the work into "player systems" and "world systems" and move fast. With four, you need finer-grained task boundaries, and those boundaries are hard to define cleanly when the game design is still evolving during the jam.

We ran into situations where two programmers both implemented solutions to the same problem in different ways, or where one person's changes to a shared system broke another person's feature. The item pickup system was particularly messy: it was touched by three of the four programmers at different points, and by submission time it had inconsistent behavior between the rat and spider because two different interaction models had been partially merged.

These two approaches both worked individually but made different assumptions about how items behave when held, how they get dropped, and how they interact with the inventory system. A single unified ItemInteraction class used by both characters would have prevented this entirely.

3. The build had a missing data folder on release

The most embarrassing issue was reported in the itch.io comments: the initial upload was missing a data folder required to run the game. This is a packaging problem, not a code problem, but the effect was the same. Players who downloaded the game could not play it. For a jam where judges have dozens of entries to evaluate and limited patience for technical issues, a game that does not launch on first try is effectively a game that does not exist.

The build pipeline for exporting a game to a distributable format is one of those things that seems straightforward until it is not. Different engines handle it differently, and the specific folder structure matters. We tested the game by running it from the editor, not from the exported build, which meant the missing data folder was never caught before submission. This is the kind of mistake you make once and never make again.

Lesson

Always test the exported build on a different machine before uploading to itch.io. Run the build from the exported folder, not the editor. If possible, have someone on the team who did not do the export download it and test it cold.

Technical Decisions Worth Examining

The cheese teleport mechanic was the spider's most interesting ability but also the hardest to implement well. When the spider player presses numpad enter, the cheese hat moves to a new location in the sewer. The question was: where does it go? We considered three approaches.

The first option was random placement anywhere on the map. This was easy to implement but felt arbitrary. The cheese would sometimes teleport to a location right next to the rat, making the ability useless. Other times it would teleport to the opposite end of the map, which felt too powerful. The second option was predetermined teleport points placed by the level designer. This was more predictable but removed the emergent gameplay that made the ability interesting. The third option, which we shipped, was weighted random placement: the cheese teleports to a random walkable tile, but the probability is weighted by distance from the rat. Tiles farther from the rat are more likely to be chosen. This meant the teleport was consistently disruptive without being randomly unfair.

GDScript cheese_teleport.gd â€” distance-weighted placement 
func teleport_cheese():
    var walkable_tiles = tilemap.get_used_cells()
    var rat_pos = rat_player.global_position
    var weighted_candidates: Array = []

    for tile in walkable_tiles:
        var world_pos = tilemap.map_to_local(tile)
        var dist = world_pos.distance_to(rat_pos)
        # Weight by distance: farther tiles are more likely
        var weight = clampf(dist / max_distance, 0.1, 1.0)
        weighted_candidates.append({
            "position": world_pos,
            "weight": weight
        })

    var chosen = _weighted_random_pick(weighted_candidates)
    cheese_hat.global_position = chosen.position
    $TeleportEffect.play()
    $TeleportSound.play()

The weighting approach was not perfect. In tight sewer layouts, the "far from the rat" constraint sometimes meant the cheese ended up in dead-end corridors that the spider could not easily defend. With more development time, we would have added a secondary filter excluding tiles with only one entrance, since those locations created stalemates where the rat could camp the corridor entrance and wait for the spider to commit to a path before rushing in. But for the jam timeline, the weighted random was good enough and the stalemates were rare enough to be acceptable.

What I Would Do Differently

If I could rerun this jam with the same team, three changes would have the highest impact. First, we would define a single item interaction interface before anyone wrote character-specific code. A shared Interactable class with pickup(), use(), and drop() methods, used identically by both the rat and spider, would have prevented the dual-implementation problem entirely.

Second, we would design both control schemes on paper before writing any input code. Mapping keys is a design problem, not a programming problem. Five minutes of whiteboarding which keys each player needs would have caught the spider's overloaded control scheme before it was built.

Third, we would build and test the export pipeline on day one. Create the itch.io page, upload a placeholder build, download it on a second machine, and verify it runs. Do this before writing a single line of game logic. The export process is boring and easy to postpone, and postponing it is exactly how you end up with a missing data folder at submission time.

Key Takeaways

Asymmetric multiplayer creates natural tension. Two characters with different toolkits produce more interesting interactions than mirrored abilities. The rat-versus-spider dynamic worked because each player had a fundamentally different experience.

Design control schemes for all players simultaneously. In local multiplayer, the second player's input mapping is not an afterthought. Design both control layouts together before writing any input handling code.

More programmers does not mean faster development. Four programmers on a jam team created coordination overhead that partially offset the additional productivity. Define clear ownership boundaries and shared interfaces before anyone starts coding.

Test the exported build, not the editor build. A game that does not launch from the downloaded archive is a game that does not get played. Test the actual deliverable on a machine that was not used for development.

Dedicated audio from day one makes a measurable difference. Sound design is not a final coat of paint. Having Shiori working on audio in parallel with programming and art gave the game a level of polish that elevated everything else.

Implementing A* Pathfinding in Godot: What the Tutorials Leave Out

Every pathfinding tutorial gives you the algorithm. None of them tell you what happens when your enemies walk through walls, stutter on corners, or freeze when the player stands on a navigation boundary.

pathfinding godot tutorial ai

I spent about two weeks implementing enemy AI for a top-down dungeon crawler as part of a course project. The enemies needed to chase the player through rooms with walls, obstacles, and narrow corridors. A* was the obvious choice. I had studied the algorithm in my CS3000 Algorithms class and understood the theory: maintain an open set, expand the node with the lowest f-cost, reconstruct the path when you reach the goal.

Getting a basic A* implementation running took about two hours. Getting it to feel right in an actual game took another twelve. This post covers the problems I ran into after the algorithm was "working" and how I solved each one.

The "working" A* implementation on day one. The enemy technically reaches the player, but clips through wall corners on diagonal moves, stutters at every grid cell transition, and occasionally vibrates in place at navigation boundaries. All three bugs had different root causes.

The Starting Point: Godot's Built-In Navigation

Godot 4.x provides a navigation system through NavigationServer2D, NavigationRegion2D, and NavigationAgent2D. For many games, this is sufficient and you should use it instead of writing your own pathfinding. I started with the built-in system and switched to a custom implementation for a specific reason: I needed enemies to react to dynamic obstacles (doors opening and closing, destructible walls) in real time, and the built-in navigation mesh rebaking was too slow for my use case.

If your obstacles are static, use Godot's built-in navigation. Do not write your own A*. I am serious. The built-in system handles path smoothing, avoidance, and edge cases that will take you days to replicate. I wrote my own because I had to, not because it was the better default choice.

Note

All code in this post uses Godot 4.3 and GDScript. The concepts apply to any A* implementation, but the specific APIs are Godot-specific.

Problem 1: Enemies Walking Through Walls

My first implementation used a grid-based approach. The dungeon is divided into cells, each cell is marked as walkable or blocked, and A* searches through walkable cells. The enemy follows the path by moving toward each waypoint in sequence. Straightforward.

The bug: enemies cut corners. When the path went around a wall corner, the enemy would move diagonally from one waypoint to the next, and that diagonal movement clipped through the wall's corner tile. The path itself was valid. The movement between path points was not.

Left: the corner-cutting problem. The A* path waypoints (green dots) are valid, but the enemy moves diagonally between them, clipping through the wall corner. Right: the fix. Cardinal-only movement prevents diagonal shortcuts entirely. The path is longer but never intersects geometry.

The fix involved two changes. First, I disabled diagonal movement in the A* grid. This forces all paths to use cardinal directions only, which eliminates corner cutting entirely at the cost of slightly less natural-looking paths. Second, I added a collision check along the movement vector before each step. If the raycast between the enemy's current position and the next waypoint hits a wall, the enemy pauses and requests a new path.

Here is the crucial part: you must restrict the A* algorithm itself to only consider 4 neighbors, not 8. Many implementations default to 8-directional movement. Simply changing the movement code is not enough if your pathfinding is still generating diagonal paths.

GDScript pathfinding.gd â€” 4-directional neighbor generation (THE FIX) 
# WRONG: 8 directions (includes diagonals)
func _get_neighbors_8dir(cell: Vector2i) -> Array[Vector2i]:
    var neighbors: Array[Vector2i] = []
    for x in range(-1, 2):
        for y in range(-1, 2):
            if x == 0 and y == 0:
                continue
            var neighbor = cell + Vector2i(x, y)
            if _is_walkable(neighbor):
                neighbors.append(neighbor)
    return neighbors  # Returns up to 8 neighbors - CAUSES CLIPPING!

# CORRECT: 4 directions only (cardinal movement)
func _get_neighbors(cell: Vector2i) -> Array[Vector2i]:
    var neighbors: Array[Vector2i] = []
    const DIRECTIONS = [
        Vector2i(0, -1),  # up
        Vector2i(0, 1),   # down
        Vector2i(-1, 0),  # left
        Vector2i(1, 0)    # right
    ]
    
    for dir in DIRECTIONS:
        var neighbor = cell + dir
        if _is_walkable(neighbor):
            neighbors.append(neighbor)
    
    return neighbors  # Only 4 cardinal neighbors - prevents diagonal paths

Problem 2: The Stutter at Every Grid Cell

Grid-based A* produces paths as a series of grid cell centers. When an enemy follows these points, it moves to the center of cell A, decelerates, changes direction, accelerates toward cell B, and repeats. This creates visible stuttering at every cell transition, especially at normal game zoom levels where individual cells are small.

The solution is path smoothing. The idea is to remove unnecessary waypoints from the path. If the enemy can move from point A directly to point C without hitting a wall, point B is unnecessary. I implemented a simple line-of-sight smoothing pass that runs after A* generates the raw path:

GDScript pathfinding.gd â€” line-of-sight path smoothing 
func smooth_path(raw_path: Array[Vector2]) -> Array[Vector2]:
    if raw_path.size() <= 2:
        return raw_path

    var smoothed: Array[Vector2] = [raw_path[0]]
    var current_index: int = 0

    while current_index < raw_path.size() - 1:
        var furthest_visible = current_index + 1

        # Find the furthest point with clear line of sight
        for i in range(current_index + 2, raw_path.size()):
            if _has_clear_line(raw_path[current_index], raw_path[i]):
                furthest_visible = i

        smoothed.append(raw_path[furthest_visible])
        current_index = furthest_visible

    return smoothed

This reduced a typical 20-waypoint corridor path to 3 or 4 waypoints. The enemy moves in longer, smoother lines and only changes direction when it actually needs to navigate around an obstacle.

Before and after path smoothing. Left: the raw A* output produces 20 waypoints through an S-shaped corridor, causing the enemy to stutter and pause at every cell transition. Right: line-of-sight smoothing collapses the path to 4 key waypoints. Each line segment has verified clear line of sight â€” no wall intersections.

Problem 3: The Recalculation Cascade

In my game, the player moves constantly. The enemy needs to update its path to follow the player. My initial approach recalculated the path every frame. This worked with one enemy. With six enemies on screen, the frame rate dropped from 60 to about 25.

A* on a 50x50 grid is not expensive for a single call. But running it six times per frame, sixty frames per second, means 360 pathfinding operations per second. Each one allocates arrays, sorts the open set, and scans neighbors. The garbage collector was working overtime.

The fix was straightforward: stagger recalculations. Each enemy recalculates its path on a timer, not every frame. I set the default interval to 0.3 seconds (roughly every 18 frames at 60fps). Additionally, enemies only recalculate if the player has moved more than two grid cells since the last calculation. If the player is standing still, enemies follow their existing paths without recalculating.

GDScript enemy_ai.gd â€” staggered path recalculation 
var repath_timer: float = 0.0
var repath_interval: float = 0.3
var last_target_cell: Vector2i = Vector2i.ZERO
const REPATH_THRESHOLD: int = 2  # grid cells

func _process(delta: float) -> void:
    repath_timer += delta
    if repath_timer < repath_interval:
        _move_along_path(delta)
        return

    repath_timer = 0.0
    var current_target_cell = grid.world_to_cell(player.global_position)

    # Only recalculate if player moved enough
    var cell_distance = (current_target_cell - last_target_cell).abs()
    if cell_distance.x >= REPATH_THRESHOLD or cell_distance.y >= REPATH_THRESHOLD:
        path = pathfinder.find_path(global_position, player.global_position)
        path = pathfinder.smooth_path(path)
        last_target_cell = current_target_cell

    _move_along_path(delta)

One additional optimization: I added a random offset to each enemy's repath_interval on spawn (repath_interval += randf_range(-0.05, 0.05)). This prevents all enemies from recalculating on the same frame, which would cause periodic frame drops every 0.3 seconds. Spreading the calculations across frames keeps the workload even.

Problem 4: The Frozen Enemy at Navigation Boundaries

This was the most confusing bug. Occasionally, an enemy would reach a certain tile and stop moving entirely. No errors in the console. The enemy's path was empty, meaning A* had returned no valid path. But the player was clearly reachable through walkable tiles.

After about an hour of debugging, I found the cause. My grid initialization marked tiles as walkable or blocked based on the TileMap's collision layer. But I was checking the wrong layer. Decorative tiles on a visual-only layer were being flagged as obstacles. The navigation grid saw walls that did not exist for physics. Specifically, torch sconce sprites placed on layer 2 (visual decorations) were being read as blockers because my grid scanner was checking all layers instead of only layer 0 (collision).

GDScript grid_builder.gd â€” the fix was one line 
# Before: scanning all layers (includes decorative sprites)
var tile_data = tilemap.get_cell_tile_data(coords)

# After: only scan the collision layer
var tile_data = tilemap.get_cell_tile_data(coords, 0)  # layer 0 only

One line of code. One hour of debugging. This is the kind of issue that no tutorial prepares you for because it depends entirely on your specific project setup. The A* algorithm was correct. The grid feeding it was wrong. When pathfinding misbehaves, check your data before checking your logic.

Performance Numbers

For reference, here is how the final implementation performs. These numbers are from a 50x50 grid (2500 cells) with approximately 30% of cells blocked, running on my laptop (Ryzen 7 5800H, 16GB RAM). A single A* path calculation with smoothing takes about 0.2ms. Six enemies with staggered recalculation produce a total pathfinding cost of roughly 0.4ms per frame on average, since only one or two enemies recalculate per frame. The built-in Godot navigation, for comparison, handles equivalent scenarios in about 0.1ms total, which is another reason to use it when you can.

Frame time breakdown with six enemies using staggered path recalculation. Pathfinding consumes 0.4ms per frame on average, roughly 2.4% of the 16.6ms budget needed for 60fps. Rendering and physics dominate as expected. Before staggering, pathfinding spiked to 1.2ms every 0.3 seconds.

Key Takeaways

Use Godot's built-in navigation first. Custom A* is only worth the effort when you need features the built-in system does not provide, like real-time dynamic obstacle updates.

Path smoothing is not optional. Raw grid paths look and feel terrible. A line-of-sight smoothing pass is the minimum requirement for acceptable movement.

Stagger expensive calculations. Never run the same expensive operation for every entity on the same frame. Add time offsets, distance thresholds, or both.

When pathfinding breaks, check the grid data first. The algorithm is almost never the problem. The data it operates on is where bugs hide.

Building a Real-Time Multiplayer Spelling Game with Socket.io: Lessons from Lexis

At LaunchHacks, our team of three built Lexis, a word-spelling game where teachers create sessions, students join, and everyone competes in real time. I handled the backend. Here is what real-time multiplayer looks like when you are learning the tools as you build with them.

The Concept

The idea for Lexis came from a simple observation: my younger brother struggled with English vocabulary because learning to spell unfamiliar words is boring in isolation. We wanted to build something that made spelling practice competitive and social. The concept we landed on was a Wordle-inspired game without Wordle's one-word-per-day limit. A teacher or parent creates a session, inputs a list of words, and students join the lobby. Each round, players hear the pronunciation of a word through text-to-speech and have to type the correct spelling. Get it right, score points. Get it wrong, lose 10 points. After all words are exhausted, a podium shows the final rankings. The name "Lexis" comes from the Greek word for "word."

Our team was three people: myself handling the entire backend and Socket.io integration, Jonah Qureshi on design and production, and Lucas Yu on frontend work (primarily the rules page and podium). Most of us had limited experience with the stack we chose. I had not used Socket.io before. Lucas was learning React and TypeScript during the hackathon. This meant we were building the project and learning the tools simultaneously, which is a common hackathon reality that rarely gets discussed in project write-ups.

The game flow for Lexis. A host creates a session with a word list, players join the lobby, then rounds cycle through each word. Text-to-speech pronounces the word, players type their guess, and scores update in real time. When all words are done, a podium with confetti shows the final rankings.

Why Socket.io

Lexis needed real-time bidirectional communication. When the host starts a round, every player needs to see the new round simultaneously. When a player submits a guess, the server needs to validate it and broadcast the updated scores to everyone. HTTP request-response does not work well for this pattern because the server has no way to push updates to clients unprompted. You would need polling, which is slow and wasteful, or server-sent events, which only go one direction.

Socket.io solves this by establishing a persistent WebSocket connection between each client and the server. Either side can send messages at any time. The server can broadcast to all connected clients, to specific rooms (our game lobbies), or to individual players. It also handles reconnection automatically, which matters when players' WiFi drops during a round.

The mental model that helped me understand Socket.io was thinking of it like a group chat. The server is the chat room. Each client has an open connection to the room. When someone sends a message, the server decides who else in the room should see it and forwards it immediately. The "rooms" feature maps directly to our game lobbies: each game session is a Socket.io room, and events within that room only reach players in that specific game.

The Architecture

The stack was React with TypeScript on the frontend, a Node.js server with Socket.io on the backend, and Chakra UI for styling. There was no database. All game state lived in server memory for the duration of the session. This was a deliberate decision for the hackathon: persistent storage was not needed because games are ephemeral. A session starts, runs through its words, shows the podium, and ends. Nothing needs to survive a server restart.

The Lexis architecture. The Node.js server manages game state in memory and uses Socket.io rooms for lobby isolation. Host and player clients are identical React apps that render different views based on role. Text-to-speech runs entirely client-side via the browser's Web Speech API. No database required for ephemeral game sessions.

The server managed four core responsibilities: room management (creating and joining lobbies), game state (tracking which word is current, which round it is, which players have answered), scoring logic (correct answers add 100 points, wrong guesses subtract 10), and event broadcasting (sending round starts, score updates, and game-over events to all players in a room).

How the Real-Time Communication Works

The event flow for a single round looks like this. The host clicks "Start" on their client. The client emits a start_round event to the server. The server looks up the current word in the session's word list, then broadcasts a round_start event to all clients in the room. That event includes the word length (but not the word itself) so the client can render the correct number of blank tiles. The client uses the browser's Web Speech API to pronounce the word, which the server sends separately in a way that only triggers the audio and does not reveal the spelling in the network payload.

// Server-side: handling a round start
io.on("connection", (socket) => {
  socket.on("start_round", (sessionId: string) => {
    const session = sessions.get(sessionId);
    if (!session) return;

    const currentWord = session.words[session.currentRound];
    const wordLength = currentWord.length;

    // Broadcast to all players in this room
    io.to(sessionId).emit("round_start", {
      round: session.currentRound,
      wordLength: wordLength,
      pronounce: currentWord  // client uses this for TTS only
    });
  });

  socket.on("submit_guess", (data: {
    sessionId: string,
    guess: string,
    playerId: string
  }) => {
    const session = sessions.get(data.sessionId);
    const correct = data.guess.toLowerCase()
      === session.words[session.currentRound].toLowerCase();

    // Update score
    const player = session.players.get(data.playerId);
    player.score += correct ? 100 : -10;

    // Broadcast updated scores to everyone
    io.to(data.sessionId).emit("score_update", {
      playerId: data.playerId,
      correct: correct,
      scores: getScoreboard(session)
    });
  });
});

When a player types a guess and submits it, their client emits a submit_guess event. The server compares the guess to the current word (case-insensitive), adjusts the player's score, and broadcasts the updated scoreboard to the entire room. Every client sees the leaderboard update in real time. This is where Socket.io's room broadcasting makes the code simple: io.to(sessionId).emit() sends the event to every socket in that room and nobody else.

What Went Wrong

1. Learning Socket.io during the hackathon

I had not used Socket.io before this project. I understood WebSockets conceptually from coursework, but the practical details of Socket.io's API (rooms, namespaces, acknowledgments, middleware) were new. The first two hours of the hackathon were spent reading documentation rather than writing code. That is a significant chunk of a hackathon timeline.

The specific issue that cost the most time was understanding Socket.io's room system. I initially tried to manage game sessions by tracking socket IDs in a custom data structure and manually filtering which sockets should receive each event. Then I discovered that Socket.io has a built-in room system where you call socket.join(roomId) and io.to(roomId).emit(). This is exactly the lobby pattern we needed. Refactoring from manual tracking to rooms took about an hour, but the resulting code was dramatically simpler.

// BEFORE: manually tracking and filtering sockets
const sessionSockets: Map<string, string[]> = new Map();

function broadcastToSession(sessionId: string, event: string, data: any) {
  const socketIds = sessionSockets.get(sessionId) || [];
  socketIds.forEach(id => {
    const s = io.sockets.sockets.get(id);
    if (s) s.emit(event, data);
  });
}

// AFTER: using built-in rooms
socket.join(sessionId);                     // player joins room
io.to(sessionId).emit(event, data);       // broadcast to room

2. Race conditions on simultaneous guesses

When multiple players submit guesses at nearly the same time, the server processes them sequentially (Node.js is single-threaded), but the score updates broadcast in rapid succession. On the client side, if two score_update events arrive within the same render frame, React would sometimes only reflect the second update, making it look like one player's guess was ignored. The score was correct on the server, but the UI showed stale data for a frame or two.

We did not fully fix this during the hackathon. The partial solution was to include the complete scoreboard (all players' scores) in every score_update event rather than just the delta for one player. This way, even if an event was missed on the client, the next event would overwrite the entire scoreboard with the correct state. It was brute-force but effective.

3. Text-to-speech inconsistency across browsers

The Web Speech API is a browser feature, not a server feature. This means pronunciation quality depends on the player's browser and operating system. Chrome on Windows sounds different from Safari on macOS. Some browsers pronounce unusual words incorrectly. One tester's browser did not support the API at all and heard nothing. We had no fallback for this case during the hackathon. A production version would need a server-side TTS service (like Google Cloud TTS) with audio streamed to clients, guaranteeing consistent pronunciation for everyone.

What I Would Build Differently

The gap between hackathon code and production code. The left column shows shortcuts that were acceptable for a demo. The right column shows what a real deployment would need. Every item on the right adds complexity but solves a failure mode that real users would hit.

The biggest architectural change would be moving game state to Redis instead of in-memory objects. Redis gives you persistence (state survives server restarts), atomic operations (no race conditions on score updates), and pub/sub (which would let us scale to multiple server instances behind a load balancer). For a hackathon with three testers, in-memory state is fine. For a classroom with 30 students, you need something more resilient.

I would also add proper reconnection handling. Socket.io reconnects automatically at the transport level, but the application needs to handle what happens when a player reconnects mid-round. Does their client know which word is active? Do they see the current scores? In our hackathon version, a reconnected player saw a blank screen until the next round started. A production version would need a "state sync" event that sends the full current game state to any reconnecting client.

The Result

Lexis won Best Design at LaunchHacks, which was primarily a credit to Jonah's design work and the Chakra UI component library making it possible to build polished UI quickly. The game flow (home page, create session, waiting room, game page, podium with confetti) felt complete and coherent even though we built it in a weekend. The confetti on the podium page, powered by the react-confetti library, was the kind of small detail that made judges smile.

The source code is on GitHub and the project page is on Devpost. Looking at the code now, months later, I can see all the hackathon shortcuts: no error handling on socket events, no input validation, no TypeScript strict mode. But the core architecture (Socket.io rooms for lobby isolation, event-driven state management, stateless clients that derive everything from server broadcasts) is sound. It is the right pattern for this kind of real-time multiplayer application, even if the implementation needs hardening.

Key Takeaways

Socket.io rooms map perfectly to game lobbies. Do not manually track which sockets belong to which session. Use socket.join(roomId) and io.to(roomId).emit(). The built-in abstraction handles edge cases you will forget about.

Send full state, not deltas, when you do not have time to build proper sync. Broadcasting the complete scoreboard on every event is wasteful but eliminates an entire class of consistency bugs. Optimize later when the core logic is proven.

Browser APIs are not consistent across platforms. If your feature depends on a browser API (Web Speech, WebGL, Bluetooth), test on at least three browser/OS combinations before the demo. Have a fallback plan.

Learning a tool during a hackathon is viable but expensive. The first two hours of reading Socket.io docs were not wasted. I learned the tool and shipped a working product. But those two hours would have been development time if I had done a tutorial project beforehand.

Hackathon code is a prototype, not a product. The gap between "works in a demo" and "works with real users" is significant. Knowing where the shortcuts are is more valuable than pretending they do not exist.
